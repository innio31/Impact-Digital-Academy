📌 Part 1: Principles of clear, concise & professional writing

Effective business writing demonstrates clear thinking and builds credibility. It is purpose‑driven: inform, persuade, instruct. All communication should rest on the 3 Cs and ideally the 7 Cs.

🔹 1.1 Clarity

A clear message leaves no doubt. The reader immediately understands purpose.

  • Know your purpose & audience: what matters to them? Tone and content follow.
  • Get to the point: state the main idea early; do not bury the ask.
  • Structure for understanding: subheadings, short paragraphs, topic sentences.
  • Precise language: avoid vague terms, jargon, clichés. Say “we will try to solve” not “endeavour to obtain a resolution”.
💡 Example: “We need the Q3 report by Friday 5 PM” instead of “We might need that report soon.”

🔹 1.2 Conciseness

Respect the reader’s time – use fewest words without cutting essentials.

  • Stick to main points; delete fluff.
  • One idea per sentence, one topic per paragraph.
  • Omit redundancies (past history, end result) and wordy phrases: replace “due to the fact that” with “because”.
  • Provide depth as an option: summary first, details attached.
✂️ wordy → concise: “at this point in time” → “now”
“in the event that” → “if”

🔹 1.3 Completeness

Include everything the reader needs to act. Leaving details out forces follow‑up and erodes trust.

  • Answer the 5 Ws: Who? What? When? Where? Why? (+ How?)
  • Anticipate reader questions: give information needed to move forward.
  • Actionable information: specify who does what by when.
Example complete request: “Alex, please draft the agenda for Monday’s 10 AM stakeholder meeting and share it by Friday COB. Include the budget update from Teresa.”

🔹 1.4 The 7 Cs of professional writing

Principle Definition Key question
Clear Easy to understand, single purpose Is main point obvious?
Concise Brief, only necessary info Can I cut any sentences?
Concrete Specific, definite, vivid Are facts & figures precise?
Correct Accurate grammar, spelling, data Any errors? Info verified?

Courteous Reader‑focused, respectful, open Is tone professional and helpful?

🔹 1.5 Practical techniques

Active voice preferred

“John submitted the report” (active) vs “The report was submitted by John” (passive).

Choose words carefully

  • Strong verbs: “recommended” not “made a recommendation”.
  • Short words: “use” not “utilize”; “begin” not “commence”.
  • Avoid clichés: “low‑hanging fruit”, “ballpark figure” – they lack impact.

Formatting for readability

Bullet points, numbered lists, bold, white space help guide the eye.

📧 Part 2: Netiquette for professional emails

Internet etiquette – professional standards for respectful, effective email communication. Every email represents you and your company.

🔹 2.1 Before you write

  • Professional email address: first.last@domain, avoid nicknames.
  • Know audience & context: formality differs for client vs colleague.

🔹 2.2 Crafting the email – structure & content

📌 Subject line

  • Always include one, be clear & specific: “Meeting rescheduled to Friday 3 PM” not “Hi”.

📌 Salutation

  • Always open with greeting: “Hello [Name]”, “Dear Mr. Smith”.
  • Introduce yourself if first contact.

📌 Email body

  • Get to the point quickly – first two sentences.
  • Concise yet complete – short paragraphs, bullet points.
  • Tone: polite (please/thank you). No sarcasm/humor, NO ALL CAPS.
  • Proper grammar & punctuation: proofread; no emojis / “LOL” / “BRB”.

📌 Closing & signature

  • Professional closing: “Sincerely”, “Best regards”, “Thank you”.
  • Signature block: full name, title, company, phone, website.
Example good email:
Subject: Question about Q3 forecast – by Friday?
Hi Pat,
Could you send the Q3 forecast numbers by Friday noon? We need them for the board pack.
Thanks,
Alex Taylor | Analyst | FinCorp | +1 555‑1234

🔹 2.3 Managing recipients & threads

  • To: people who must act. CC: people who need to know, no action.
  • BCC: use to protect privacy (large lists) or discreetly include someone.
  • Reply All sparingly: only if everyone truly needs your reply. “Thanks” not for all.
  • Threads: keep recipient list tight; update subject if topic drifts.

🔹 2.4 Final checks and sending

  • Proofread aloud – spelling, grammar, tone.
  • Check “To” field – avoid autofill mistakes.
  • Attachments: did you actually attach the file?
  • Never send when emotional – draft, save, re‑read later.
  • Respond promptly – within 24h; if delayed, acknowledge and set expectation.
⚠️ Warning signs in emails: unclear subject, missing greeting, rambling sentences, typos, missing attachment. Always double‑check.
