Education Is What Happens Inside You,
Not Around You

Part 2: The Classroom Is Not a Prison (Why Students Check Out and How to Invite Them Back In)

✍️ by Emmanuel Ademuyiwa

A Story That Will Hurt a Little

Mr. Adebayo has been teaching for 17 years. He knows his subject—Government—like the back of his hand. He arrives at school before the bell. His lesson notes are always ready. The principal calls him "dedicated."

But Mr. Adebayo has a problem.

His students are not there.

I mean, physically, they are there. They sit in the chairs. They open their notebooks. They even nod sometimes. But if you look into their eyes—really look—you'll see they've traveled to another country. Some are in Lagos. Some are in London. Some are in a dream where they're eating amala without having to share.

Mr. Adebayo gets angry about this. He shouts. He sends students out. He says things like, "You students of nowadays have no respect! In our time, we listened!"

But here's the painful truth Mr. Adebayo doesn't want to face: you cannot force someone to learn.

You can force them to sit. You can force them to be quiet. You can force them to copy notes. But you cannot force learning to happen inside them. Learning is not a command. It's an invitation. And if your classroom feels like a prison, nobody is accepting your invitation.

The Great Escape: Where Do Students Go When They Leave Your Class?

Let me ask you something uncomfortable.

Think about your most disengaged student. The one at the back. The one drawing in their notebook. The one who always has their head down.

Where do you think they go when they mentally check out?

Maybe they're thinking about what happened at home this morning—the fight between mum and dad, the fact that there was no food for breakfast, the younger sibling they had to take care of before coming to school.

Maybe they're thinking about the future—the WASSCE that's approaching like a train, the pressure to get into university, the fear of becoming another "sitting at home" graduate.

Maybe they're thinking about money—the school fees that are still owed, the uniform that's too small, the shoes with the sole coming off.

Maybe they're thinking about love—the crush who doesn't know they exist, the friend who betrayed them, the loneliness of feeling unseen.

Here's what I'm saying: your students are carrying heavy things. And when you stand at the front and lecture about the structure of local government or the formula for calculating relative density, those heavy things are still there. They don't disappear because the bell rang.

So whose job is it to lighten the load?

The Difference Between Teaching and Imprisoning

I want you to think about the difference between a prison and a home.

In a prison, everything is controlled. Movement is restricted. Questions are discouraged. The goal is compliance. You do what you're told, when you're told, or there are consequences.

In a home, there is structure, yes. But there is also warmth. There is also belonging. People move freely. They ask questions. They contribute. The goal is not just compliance—it's growth.

Now ask yourself: what does your classroom feel like?

Be honest.

When students walk in, do they relax? Do they breathe? Do they feel like they belong? Or do they tighten up, put on their mask, and prepare to survive the next 40 minutes?

I'm not saying we should turn classrooms into playgrounds. Structure matters. Discipline matters. But if the only thing students feel when they enter your room is fear, then nothing good is going to grow there.

Why Students Check Out: The Nigerian Edition

Let's get specific about why our students—Nigerian students in Nigerian classrooms—check out.

Reason 1: The Class Is Too Full
You have 80, 100, sometimes 150 students in one room. You can't even walk between the desks. You're shouting to be heard. Individual attention? Abeg, who get time? In this situation, it's easy for a student to feel invisible. And when you feel invisible, why would you participate? Why would you pay attention? Nobody will notice if you're gone anyway.

Reason 2: The Teaching Is Too Abstract
"You will learn this because it's in the syllabus." "You will learn this because it will come out in exam." "You will learn this because I said so." These are the reasons we give. And they are terrible reasons. If I'm 14 years old and struggling to eat one meal a day, do you think I care about the history of the Berlin Conference? If I'm worried about my pregnant teenage sister who was sent away from home, do you think I'm focused on the properties of amines? We teach things that have no connection to the lives students are actually living. And then we wonder why they're not interested.

Reason 3: The Method Is Torture
Let me paint a picture. Enter: class. Teacher: "Open your notebook." Students open notebook. Teacher: "Copy this note." Teacher dictates. Students copy. For 40 minutes. Teacher: "Close your notebook. Next week, we will do revision. The following week, test." This happens every day. In thousands of classrooms across Nigeria. Is it any wonder students are bored? Is it any wonder they'd rather be on TikTok or Instagram? At least those apps talk to them. At least those apps show them things that matter to them.

Reason 4: They Don't Feel Safe
Here's something we don't talk about enough. For many students, school is not a safe place. Maybe there's a teacher who humiliates them. "You're so dull. You'll never pass. Your mates are far ahead of you." Maybe there's a bully who intimidates them. On the way to school. During break. Even in class when the teacher isn't looking. Maybe there's pressure from home. "If you don't come top of the class, you're not my child." When you're in survival mode—when your main goal is to avoid pain—learning becomes impossible. Your brain is too busy protecting you to absorb new information.

Reason 5: They've Given Up
This is the saddest one. Some of our students have been told so many times that they're stupid, that they're failures, that they'll never amount to anything, that they've started believing it. They've stopped trying because trying hurts. When you try and fail, it confirms what everyone says about you. But if you don't try, you can tell yourself, "I could have passed if I tried." It's a protection mechanism. And we, as teachers, often make it worse.

What Research Says About This

Those researchers in Niger State who studied flipped classrooms found something interesting. When students are passive—just sitting and listening—their brains are barely active. But when they have to engage, when they have to participate, when they have to solve problems, their brains light up like Christmas.

Another study in Oyo State looked at why students drop out. You know what they found? It wasn't just poverty. It wasn't just distance to school. It was boredom. It was irrelevance. It was feeling like school had nothing to offer them.

And yet another study on digital learning in Borno showed that when students have some control over their learning—when they can go at their own pace, when they can choose what to focus on—they don't drop out. They lean in.

The lesson is clear: students don't check out because they're lazy. They check out because we haven't given them a reason to stay.

How to Invite Them Back In: Practical Strategies

Okay. Enough diagnosis. Let's talk about treatment. How do we transform our classrooms from prisons into places where students actually want to be?

1
Shrink the Class, Even When You Can't
You have 100 students. You can't change that. But you can break them into smaller groups. Instead of teaching to the whole mass, create clusters. Give each cluster a problem to solve. Let them talk to each other. Let them teach each other. When students work in small groups, something magical happens. The ones who were hiding become visible. The ones who were silent find their voice. The ones who were lost find help from their peers. You don't need government to reduce class size for this to work. You just need to be intentional.
2
Start With Their World, Not With the Syllabus
Before you teach any topic, ask yourself: how does this connect to my students' lives? Teaching about rivers? Ask: "Which river is near your village? What do people use it for?" Teaching about elections? Ask: "In your compound, how do people choose who gets the last piece of meat?" Teaching about fractions? Ask: "If you have ₦200 and you need to share it with your two younger siblings, how much does each person get? Is that fair?" When you start with what they know, they feel respected. They feel seen. And once they feel seen, they're ready to learn.
3
Change the Rhythm
Nobody—not even the most dedicated professor—can focus for 40 minutes straight. The human brain isn't designed for that. So don't lecture for 40 minutes. Break it up. 5 minutes: Hook them with a story or question. 10 minutes: Explain the core idea. 10 minutes: Group activity or discussion. 5 minutes: Students share what they discussed. 5 minutes: Quick write or reflection. 5 minutes: Preview what's coming next. Different activities invite different kinds of participation.
4
Make It Safe to Be Wrong
This is huge. In most Nigerian classrooms, being wrong is dangerous. But learning requires being wrong. When a student gives a wrong answer, try saying: "Interesting. Tell me how you arrived at that." "That's not what I expected. Let's think through this together." "Who else had a different thought? Let's compare." When students see that wrong answers lead to conversation instead of punishment, they start taking risks. And when they take risks, they start learning.
5
Know Their Names
This sounds small. It's not. In a class of 100, it's hard to know everyone's name. But try. Make a seating plan. Use it every lesson. Learn five new names each day. When you call a student by name, you're saying: "I see you. You're not invisible." When you remember something about them—"Chidi, how did your sister's naming ceremony go?"—you're saying: "You matter beyond this classroom." These small gestures build trust. And trust is the foundation of learning.
6
Give Them Choice
Prisoners have no choices. Free people do. Where can you give your students choice? Choice of topic for an assignment. Choice of partner for group work. Choice of how to present what they've learned (write, draw, act, sing). Choice of which questions to answer first. Even small choices make a difference. They give students a sense of control. And when students feel in control, they're more likely to engage.
7
Tell Stories
Facts are forgettable. Stories stick. Instead of just listing the causes of the Nigerian Civil War, tell the story of someone who lived through it. Instead of just explaining photosynthesis, tell the story of how a farmer's yam grows. Stories create pictures in the mind. They trigger emotions. And when emotion is present, memory improves. We are storytelling animals. Use that.

A Story From the Classroom

There's a teacher I know in Ibadan. Let's call her Mrs. Oluchi.

She taught English in a secondary school where most students hated the subject. They saw it as difficult, foreign, irrelevant. They failed consistently. They'd given up.

One day, instead of teaching from the textbook, Mrs. Oluchi brought something different to class. She brought a recording of Fela's "Suffering and Smiling."

She played it for the students. Then she asked: "What is Fela saying? What words does he use? How does he arrange them? Why does it stick in your head even after one listen?"

The class came alive. Students who never spoke were arguing about lyrics. Students who couldn't identify a verb were analyzing Fela's word choices.

From there, Mrs. Oluchi moved to other Nigerian musicians—Burna Boy, Simi, Asake. Students analyzed lyrics. They discussed meaning. They debated whether music counts as poetry.

By the end of term, those same students were writing their own poems. In English. Willingly.

What changed? Not the syllabus. Not the textbook. Mrs. Oluchi found a door that was already open—their love for music—and walked through it.

She invited them in. And they came.

What This Means for You

I know what some of you are thinking.

"This is all well and good, but you don't know my school. You don't know my students. You don't know the pressure I'm under to finish the syllabus. You don't know how tired I am."

You're right. I don't know your specific situation.

But here's what I do know: the students in front of you today are the only students you have. They deserve your best, even when the conditions are not ideal. They deserve to be invited, even when the system treats them like prisoners.

And here's the thing: when you start treating them differently, you'll feel different too. Teaching becomes less exhausting when students are actually with you instead of against you. The days go faster when there's laughter and conversation instead of silence and fear. The job becomes lighter when you're not fighting students every step of the way.

The Questions We Must Ask Ourselves

For you, teacher

  • If you were a student in your own class, would you look forward to coming?
  • When was the last time you laughed with your students?
  • Do your students feel safer inside your classroom than outside it?
  • What would your students say about you if you weren't in the room?
  • Which student have you given up on? And what would it take to believe in them again?

For your students

  • "Does my teacher know my name?"
  • "Can I be wrong in this class without being punished?"
  • "Does what we're learning matter to my life?"
  • "Does anyone here see me?"

A Final Thought

Habiba, the girl from Borno who wants to be a doctor—the one who said "Now, I don't give up"—she didn't give up because someone forced her. She didn't give up because the government fixed all the problems.

She kept going because something shifted inside her. Because she found a reason to believe.

That reason might have been a digital learning tool. It might have been a teacher who saw her. It might have been a moment when she realized she could do it.

We may never know exactly what it was.

But here's what I know: every day, in classrooms across Nigeria, we have the chance to be that reason for someone.

Not by being perfect. Not by having all the answers. Not by waiting for the government to fix everything.

But by creating classrooms where students feel seen, where they feel safe, where they feel invited.

Classrooms that are not prisons.

Classrooms that are homes.
