A Confession to Start

Let me tell you about my worst year of teaching.

It was my fifth year. I had mastered the curriculum. I could recite the scheme of work in my sleep. I had notes for every topic, saved on my phone, ready to print. I walked into class confident, even cocky.

But something was wrong.

The fire was gone.

I taught the same lessons the same way I had taught them the year before, and the year before that. I used the same jokes. I told the same stories. I even wrote on the board in the same order—I knew exactly where the chalk would hit.

My students were passing. The results were fine. Nobody was complaining.

But I was bored. And if I was bored, my students must have been dying.

That year, I learned a painful truth: you cannot take your students where you are not going yourself.

If you have stopped growing, how can you help your students grow? If you have stopped learning, how can you inspire learning? If you have stopped asking questions, how can you teach your students to ask questions?

You can't. It's like a dead man trying to raise children. E no work.

The Nigerian Teacher's Reality: Why Growth Is Hard

Before we go further, let's be honest about the obstacles.

Obstacle 1: The System Is Not Helping You
When was the last time you attended a training that actually helped you?

Not the one where they brought "consultants" who read PowerPoint slides for three hours. Not the one where they talked about theory while you sat on a hard bench, sweating, thinking about the lessons you were supposed to be preparing.

Real professional development—the kind that changes how you teach—is rare in Nigeria. According to research, only about 10% of Nigerian teachers are considered highly trained. In Zamfara and Sokoto, over 50% of teachers have never seen any form of professional development at all.

The system is not investing in you. That's the truth.

Obstacle 2: You're Exhausted
You're teaching 40 periods a week. You're marking 200 notebooks. You're attending meetings that could have been emails. You're dealing with parents who blame you for everything. You're worried about your own salary, your own family, your own future.

By Friday, you're running on empty. The last thing you have energy for is "personal development."

I understand. I've been there.

Obstacle 3: Nobody Is Rewarding Growth
Let's be real. In many Nigerian schools, nobody cares if you're growing.

Do you get paid more for learning new teaching methods? No.
Do you get promoted for reading educational books? No.
Does anyone check if you're still excited about teaching? No.

The only thing that matters is exam results. And if your students are passing—even by cheating—nobody asks questions.

So why bother? Why add more to your plate when nobody is watching?

These are fair questions. They deserve honest answers.

But Here's Why You Must Grow Anyway

Because you are not just a worker. You are a human being.

And human beings are not designed to stay the same. We are designed to grow. When growth stops, something inside us starts to die.

You feel it, don't you? That heaviness on Sunday evening when you realize tomorrow is Monday. That feeling of going through the motions. That sense that you're just waiting for retirement, or for "japa," or for something to change.

That's not just burnout. That's your soul telling you that you need to grow.

What Happens When Teachers Stop Growing

Let me paint you a picture of a teacher who has stopped growing.

We'll call her Mrs. Ekaette.

Mrs. Ekaette has been teaching for 22 years. She knows her subject. She has her methods. She doesn't need anyone to tell her how to teach.

When new ideas come—maybe about group work, maybe about technology, maybe about making lessons more engaging—Mrs. Ekaette waves them away.

"These new methods are just noise. In my time, we taught properly. These children of nowadays just want to play. Let them suffer like we suffered."

Mrs. Ekaette's classroom is quiet. Her students are afraid of her. They pass their exams because they memorize everything. But ask them a question that requires thinking, and they freeze.

Mrs. Ekaette doesn't see the problem. She thinks she's maintaining standards. She thinks she's protecting education from "soft" approaches.

But here's the truth: Mrs. Ekaette has become a monument. She's not a teacher anymore. She's just repeating herself, year after year, while the world changes around her.

The students she's "preparing" for the future are being trained for a past that no longer exists.

The Other Picture: What Growth Looks Like

Now let me tell you about Mr. Emeka.

Mr. Emeka has also been teaching for over 20 years. But something is different about him.

Last year, he learned how to use WhatsApp to create study groups for his students. This year, he's experimenting with "flipped classroom"—recording short videos for students to watch at home so they can do activities in class.

He attends every training he can find, even the free ones online. He reads books about teaching. He tries things, fails, adjusts, tries again.

His students don't just pass exams—they actually understand. They ask questions. They argue with him. They come back after they've graduated to thank him.

When you ask Mr. Emeka why he still bothers after all these years, he laughs.

"I'm still learning," he says. "Every year, I discover something new. Every class teaches me something. If I ever stop learning, I might as well retire."

Mr. Emeka is not a monument. He's a garden. He's still growing.

What Research Says About Teacher Growth

Those researchers studying education in Nigeria have found something consistent: teacher quality is the single biggest factor in student learning. More than class size. More than textbooks. More than technology.

A well-trained, motivated teacher can work miracles with limited resources. A burnt-out, stagnant teacher can waste even the best resources.

And here's what's interesting: the teachers who keep growing—who keep learning—are also the ones who stay in the profession longer and report more satisfaction.

Growth is not just good for students. It's good for you. It keeps you alive.

How to Grow When Nobody Is Helping You

Okay. You're convinced. You want to grow. But the system isn't helping. You're tired. You're busy. What do you actually do?

Here are practical strategies that cost little or nothing.

1
Read Something Every Term
Just one book. Or even half a book. But make it about teaching.

Not a textbook. Not something boring. Something that will actually help you.

  • "Teach Like a Champion" by Doug Lemov (practical classroom techniques)
  • "The First Days of School" by Harry Wong (for classroom management)
  • "Mindset" by Carol Dweck (about how beliefs affect learning)
  • "Why Don't Students Like School?" by Daniel Willingham (based on cognitive science)

If you can't afford books, use the internet. Search for summaries. Watch YouTube videos. Follow Nigerian education blogs. There's free information everywhere. You just need to look.

2
Find Your People
Teaching can be lonely. You're in your classroom all day, surrounded by students but isolated from other adults.

Find other teachers who care about growing.

Maybe it's two or three people in your school. Maybe it's a WhatsApp group with teachers from other schools. Maybe it's an online community.

Talk about what's working. Share your struggles. Ask for advice. Celebrate small wins.

When you have people walking with you, the journey feels lighter.

3
Try One New Thing Per Term
You don't need to revolutionize everything at once. That's how you burn out.

Just pick one thing. One new strategy. One new approach.

This term, try group work. Next term, try using more questions. The term after, try letting students choose their own topics for an assignment.

Experiment. See what works. Adjust. Try again.

Small changes, over time, add up to transformation.

4
Learn From Your Students
This might sound strange, but your students are some of your best teachers.

Ask them: "What did you enjoy about this lesson? What was boring? What would you change?"

You don't have to do everything they say. But listen. Pay attention. Notice which lessons they engage with and which ones make them sleepy.

Students will tell you what's working, if you're humble enough to ask.

5
Reflect Every Week
Just 10 minutes. On Friday after school, or Sunday evening.

Ask yourself:

  • What went well this week?
  • What flopped?
  • What did I learn about my students?
  • What did I learn about myself?
  • What will I do differently next week?

Write it down if you can. Not for anyone else. Just for you.

Reflection turns experience into learning. Without reflection, you just keep doing the same things, even if they're not working.

6
Use Your Phone for Good, Not Just for Distraction
Yes, we all waste time on social media. But your phone can also be a learning tool.

Follow educational pages. Join teacher groups. Watch teaching videos on YouTube during your break. Listen to podcasts while you're commuting.

There are Nigerian teachers on social media sharing real strategies that work in our context. Find them. Learn from them.

7
Say Yes to Opportunities
When a training comes—even if it's not perfect—say yes.
When someone invites you to a workshop—even if it's far—say yes.
When there's a chance to observe another teacher—even if you're busy—say yes.

Opportunities to grow are everywhere. But you have to be open to them. You have to be humble enough to receive.

What Growth Does to Your Classroom

When you start growing again, things shift.

Your lessons become fresher. You're not just repeating last year's notes—you're trying new things. That energy is contagious.

Your students notice. They see that you're still learning, still trying, still growing. And that gives them permission to do the same.

Your relationship with them changes. When you're not just the "expert" but also a learner, you become more human to them. More approachable. More real.

And your own satisfaction increases. Teaching stops being a chore and starts being an adventure again. You wake up on Monday morning with something to look forward to—not just another week of survival, but a chance to try that new idea you've been thinking about.

A Story From the North

A Story From the North

Remember Habiba from Borno? The girl who wants to be a doctor?

Her teacher, Mallam Umar, is part of this story too.

When digital learning came to their school, Mallam Umar could have resisted. He's been teaching for years. He knows his subject. Why should he learn some new technology?

But Mallam Umar did something different. He learned.

He sat with the tablets. He figured out how they worked. He let his students teach him things. He used the new tools alongside his old methods.

Did it take time? Yes.
Was it frustrating sometimes? Yes.
Did he make mistakes? Of course.

But here's what happened: his students saw him learning. They saw him struggle and keep going. They saw that learning doesn't stop when you become an adult.

And that example—that living example of a grown man still willing to be a beginner—might have been more powerful than any lesson he taught.

The Danger of Pride

Let me be direct with you.

The biggest enemy of teacher growth is pride.

"I already know how to teach."
"I've been doing this for 20 years."
"These new methods are nonsense."
"Nobody can tell me anything about my subject."

This pride is understandable. You've worked hard. You've paid your dues. You deserve respect.

But here's the thing: pride closes the door to growth. When you think you already know everything, you stop learning. And when you stop learning, you start dying—slowly, as a teacher.

The best teachers I know are not the ones who know everything. They're the ones who know they don't know everything. They're curious. They're humble. They're always asking, "What can I learn next?"

What About When You're Truly Tired?

I hear you. You're exhausted. You're underpaid. You're overworked. The last thing you need is someone telling you to do more.

So let me be clear: growth doesn't have to mean more work. It can mean different work.

Sometimes, growth is not about adding. It's about subtracting.

Maybe you need to grow by letting go of methods that aren't working. Maybe you need to grow by delegating more to students. Maybe you need to grow by taking better care of yourself so you have more energy for teaching.

Growth is not just about doing more. It's about becoming more. And sometimes becoming more means resting more, so you can show up fully when you're teaching.

Questions for Your Own Heart

Before we end, I want to leave you with some questions. Take them seriously.

  • When was the last time you learned something new about teaching?
  • What's one area of your teaching that you know needs improvement?
  • Who is helping you grow? Who are your people?
  • What's stopping you from growing? Be honest.
  • If you keep teaching the way you're teaching now for the next 10 years, will you be proud of yourself?

These questions are not to shame you. They're to wake you up.

The Legacy of a Growing Teacher

Think about the teachers who changed your life.

I'm willing to bet they weren't perfect. They probably made mistakes. They probably didn't know everything.

But they were alive. They were present. They cared about you. They believed in you. And they were still growing themselves.

That's the kind of teacher we all want to be. That's the kind of teacher our students deserve.

And here's the beautiful thing: it's never too late to start.

You can start today. This week. This term.

Read one article. Try one new strategy. Have one honest conversation with a colleague. Ask your students one question about how you can improve.

Small steps. Consistent steps. That's how growth happens.

A Final Word

Education is what happens inside you, not around you.

That's true for your students. And it's true for you.

The classroom can be full of resources. The government can finally pay salaries on time. The curriculum can be perfect. But if nothing is happening inside you—if you're not growing, not learning, not becoming more—then your students will feel it.

They will sense that something is missing.

But if you're growing—if you're curious, if you're trying, if you're becoming more fully yourself every year—then your students will feel that too.

They will catch your curiosity like a fever.
They will absorb your love of learning like sunshine.
They will become, without even realizing it, people who never stop growing.

Because that's what teachers do. We don't just teach subjects. We teach ourselves. We teach who we are.

So who are you becoming?

The answer to that question is the most important lesson you will ever teach.
